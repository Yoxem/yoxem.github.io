(updated on 2019-04-16)

Broad and slender

In all the original consonants in Irish, except /h/, itвҖҷs distinguished between slender one (palatalized) and broad one (velarized), and those which are next to a broad vowel are broad consonants, while consonants adjacent to a slender vowel are slender consonants.

    Stressed vs. unstressed

    Syllables in Irish are stressed, unstressed, and secondary-stressed (only in compound words). Unstressed short monophthong (a, e, i, o, u) are usually reduced to schwa /Йҷ/. For the stress rule, see Section Word stress rule.

    The unstressed schwa /Йҷ/ between slender vowels is sound like /ЙӘ/.

    Vowel + bh, dh, gh, mh

    As time went by, syllables in an Old Irish words might combined into a syllable, and some consonants became slient. However, like the historical kana orthography of Japanese, the orthography of Modern Irish still reflects the historical spelling instead of the modern pronounciation in some degree, even if the new orthography where some slient consonants are removed has been promoted in 20th century. Therefore, there are rules some lenited consonants with the preceding vowel that learners have to memorize.

    Besides, new vowels that are a combination with вҖңmhвҖқ will be nasalized(/в—ҢМғ/) by some speaker.

    Word stress rule

    According to Teach Yourself Irish (1961), the stress of a non-compound word is usually on the 1st stress, except the 3 rules:

    • Except some verbal endings, if the 2nd syllable is long, the stress is on the 2nd syllable. eg.: cailГӯn.
    • If the 1st & 2nd syllables are short and the 3rd syllable is long, the stress is on the 3rd one. eg.: amadГЎn.
    • If the 2nd syllable has -ach ending, and there is no long syllable, the stress is on the 2nd syllable. eg. casachtach.
